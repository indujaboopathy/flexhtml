The Roman emperors were the rulers of the Roman Empire from the granting of the title Augustus to Octavian (pictured) in 27 BC. The English word emperor derives from imperator, an ancient honorific that served as one of the several titles of the emperor. Early emperors ruled behind a republican facade and avoided monarchical titles, but this changed over the following centuries, after which the position became more akin to an absolute monarchy. After the Crisis of the Third Century, the empire was split into a Greek East and Latin West, each with its own emperor.
